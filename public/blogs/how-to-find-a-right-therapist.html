How to Find the Right Therapist

By The Mindful Heart Team | April 2026

If you found this blog and are reading this, there's a high chance that you are looking for a right and definitely a good therapist. Maybe this is your first time seeking therapy and don't know what to look for in a therapist or you've had an experience with a therapist but that didn't work out for some reason. We want to appreciate you here for taking the first step towards figuring that out.

What "Good" or "right" stands for you is very subjective. Does good means someone who has higher qualifications/training or they are recommended by many people or who makes you feel safe and warm? Just take a moment and pause and see what good means for you and write that down. There's no right or wrong answer.

Apart from your subjective criteria for finding a therapist, here are a few other factors that we recommend to consider while looking for a therapist:

Key Factors to Consider

1. Qualifications

In India, a practising psychologist and psychotherapist must hold a master's degree in psychology to do so. They may hold any other higher degree like PhD or MPhil. But a master's is a minimum requirement. For example, a therapist may hold a master's degree in Counselling Psychology, Clinical Psychology, or Industrial Psychology.

Myth: The higher the degree the better the therapist is.
Fact: A Therapist may hold PhD but that doesn't necessarily define or give a complete picture of their clinical skills.

2. Area of Expertise

This consists of the mental health area that the therapist holds expertise through training and years of experiences in working and supporting through. For example: A therapist who holds expertise in working with Anxiety and anxiety-related disorders. This can be helpful while you are looking for a therapist. Check whether the therapist holds expertise in the concern area you are seeking help for.

Myth: The therapist should have gone through the same experience then only they can support me better and understand me.
Fact: For therapy to be effective, therapists need not necessarily have to have the same experience. (It's like saying an oncologist who has had cancer can only cure a cancer patient effectively).

3. Population Expertise

This is another important factor to consider. Sometimes, a therapist may hold an expertise in a particular area but not in a specific population that you may fall under. For example: If you are looking for a therapist to support an 8-year-old child who's been diagnosed with ADHD and the therapist specialised in ADHD but works only with adult population might not be the right fit. Hence it matters which population the therapist works with.

4. Affirmative Approach

In our country like India where there are multiple social, economic, political, cultural factors that play an important role in impacting people's lives. It's important to consider whether your therapist is Multiculturally affirmative, Neurodivergent affirmative, LGBTQIA+ affirmative, caste-affirmative therapist or caste-aware practitioner. Specifically, if your concern area is impacted by system/Macro context to offer care that takes a holistic approach into account.

5. Years of Experience

This is one factor that many of us consider when seeking services from any professional, whether they are a healthcare professional or from another field. Therapists with more years of clinical experience have spent more time working with individuals and supporting a range of mental health concerns. As a result, many people assume that greater experience directly translates to stronger therapeutic skills.

Years of experience can certainly provide opportunities for therapists to further develop their clinical skills, deepen their knowledge, and build confidence in the areas they work with. At the same time, years of experience alone do not determine how effective a therapist will be. Budding therapists are at the beginning of their professional journey and naturally have fewer years of clinical experience. However, this does not mean they are unable to provide effective support. They have completed the required education and training, and many continue to receive regular supervision and guidance as they develop their practice. After all, every experienced therapist was once a budding therapist.

Myth: The greater the year of experience, the better the therapist is.
Fact: Years of experience can be one factor to understand the therapist being a good fit but it's not necessarily an indicator or proof of skills of a therapist.

6. Languages Known

Language is a way of expression of our inner and our world. We define and express our thoughts, our innermost emotions and give words to our struggles and joy. Hence we would want our therapist to really understand us. India is a multilingual country where people are multilingual. You can look for a therapist in the language that you are comfortable in.

Myth: I will have to take sessions in English if I reach out to a therapist.
Fact: Therapists in India take sessions in multiple languages. You can access a therapist who takes sessions in the language you are comfortable in, that can be English or your mother tongue.

7. Therapeutic Approach/Modality

Therapeutic approach consists of an evidence-based way to understand mental health concern as well as support them with that understanding. There are many different psychotherapies available that range from focusing and working with people with their thoughts and beliefs like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT) to approaches focusing primarily on people's emotions like Accelerated Experiential Dynamic Psychotherapy (AEDP), Emotion-Focused Therapy (EFT). Many therapists are trained and use more than one modality. There are therapy approaches that involve non-verbal or more than just talking like Play therapy that uses play to understand and support your mental health concern to Art therapy that does the same using art as a tool. You can always ask your therapist what approach they primarily use and what that may look like while supporting you with that.

Myth: There's one best therapy and that can only be the most effective.
Fact: All therapeutic approaches are evidence-based and some are proven to be more effective than other approaches for a mental health concern. But there's no right or best approach, there are a lot of variables that play a role while working with one individual.

8. Finance/Budget for Therapy

Therapy sessions are usually charged per session rather than the whole treatment costs unlike we pay for treatment with other health care professionals. Therapy sessions are offered between 500 rs to 5000 rs per session based on a therapist's/organisation's area of residence. Urban cities in India may have higher cost compared to suburban or rural parts of residence. Some therapists offer sliding scale - in case if you are in a financial constraint you can check with the therapist if they do offer sliding scale slots.

9. In-person or Online

Some therapists primarily conduct sessions online through secure video platforms such as Google Meet or Zoom, while others offer therapy in a physical clinical setting where you can meet them face-to-face. Many therapists also provide both options. You can explore which mode works best for you based on your preferences, comfort, accessibility, and practical needs, and discuss any questions or concerns you may have with your therapist before starting therapy.

Myth: In-person therapy is always the best option.
Fact: The effectiveness of therapy depends on several factors, including the therapeutic relationship, the therapist's competence, the client's needs and preferences, and consistent engagement in the therapeutic process. For many mental health concerns, research suggests that online therapy can be just as effective as in-person therapy. However, one mode may be more suitable than the other depending on an individual's circumstances and the nature of the concerns they are seeking support for.

10. Therapist-Client Match/Fit

Therapy is a collaborative process, and research indicates that the therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of positive therapy outcomes. Therefore, it is important that you and your therapist develop a comfortable and trusting working relationship. Sometimes, a therapist may have extensive experience and be highly competent, yet the therapeutic fit may not feel right for you. It may simply not click.

If this happens, it can be helpful to first discuss your experience openly with your therapist and see whether the concern can be worked through together. Many therapists encourage clients to share when something feels off, as these conversations can strengthen the therapeutic relationship and improve the therapy process. If, despite these discussions, the fit still doesn't feel right, you can communicate this with your therapist and request a referral to another therapist if needed.

11. Word of Mouth/Referrals

Another factor to consider is seeking a referral. Referrals may come from healthcare professionals, such as your physician, psychiatrist, or another mental health professional who is familiar with a therapist's work. They may also come from someone who has had direct experience receiving therapy from that therapist.

A referral can be a helpful starting point when looking for a therapist. If you feel comfortable doing so, you can ask friends, family members, or colleagues whether they know of a therapist who may be able to support you with your concerns. However, keep in mind that a therapist who is a good fit for one person may not necessarily be the right fit for another, so it is still important to consider other factors, such as the therapist's expertise, approach, and your own comfort and connection with them.

Conclusion

I hope these pointers are helpful for you to consider while making a decision to choose a therapist. The above are a few guidelines or pointers from a fellow therapist and not some set rules. There's no one right way or correct way.
